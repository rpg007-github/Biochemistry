01 Amino Acids & Protein Structure

There are 20 amino acids. They all share the same backbone (amino group, carboxyl group, alpha carbon), but each has a unique side chain (R-group) that determines its properties. The R-group is everything. It controls whether the amino acid is hydrophobic, polar, positively charged, or negatively charged.

The Four R-Group Categories

GroupCharacterExamples
NonpolarOily, avoids water, buries inside proteinsAla (A), Val (V), Leu (L), Ile (I), Phe (F), Pro (P)
Polar unchargedCan hydrogen-bond with water, no formal chargeSer (S), Thr (T), Asn (N), Gln (Q), Cys (C), Tyr (Y)
Positive (+) at pH 7.4Basic side chainsLys (K, pKa ~10.5), Arg (R, pKa ~12.5), His (H, pKa ~6.0)
Negative (-) at pH 7.4Acidic side chainsAsp (D, pKa ~3.65), Glu (E, pKa ~4.25)
Common Mistake
"Nonpolar" and "uncharged" are not the same thing. Serine is polar and uncharged: it has no formal charge but still hydrogen-bonds with water. Leucine is nonpolar: it cannot even do that. It actively avoids water. Many students lose points by treating these as the same category.

Calculating the Isoelectric Point (pI)

The pI is the pH where the amino acid has zero net charge. For a simple amino acid (no ionizable side chain), average the two backbone pKa values.

Worked Example: pI of Glycine

Glycine has pKa1 = 2.34 (carboxyl) and pKa2 = 9.60 (amino). No ionizable side chain.

1.pI = (2.34 + 9.60) / 2 = 5.97

At pH 5.97, glycine has no net charge.

Worked Example: pI of Aspartate (acidic amino acid)

Asp has pKa1 = 2.10 (carboxyl), pKaR = 3.86 (side chain), pKa2 = 9.82 (amino). Which two do you average?

1.Asp is acidic (extra COOH), so the two lowest pKa values bracket the zwitterion.
2.pI = (2.10 + 3.86) / 2 = 2.98

Rule: acidic amino acids have LOW pI (average the two lowest). Basic amino acids have HIGH pI (average the two highest).

Protein Structure Levels

Primary = the amino acid sequence itself. Secondary = local folding patterns (alpha-helix and beta-sheet) held by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms. Tertiary = the overall 3D shape of one polypeptide, driven mainly by hydrophobic interactions burying nonpolar residues inside. Quaternary = multiple polypeptide subunits assembled together (like hemoglobin, which has four subunits).

Worked Example: Sickle Cell Disease Step by Step

Sickle cell is caused by one amino acid change: glutamate (charged, hydrophilic) to valine (nonpolar, hydrophobic) at position 6 of the beta-globin chain.

1.Glu → Val creates a hydrophobic sticky patch on the surface of hemoglobin.
2.Under low O₂ conditions (deoxy-Hb), this patch is exposed.
3.That sticky patch binds to a complementary pocket on another hemoglobin molecule.
4.Hemoglobin molecules chain together into long fibers, distorting the red blood cell into a sickle shape.

One amino acid change at the primary level cascades into a quaternary-level aggregation problem and a clinical disease. This is the clearest example of why primary structure matters.

Memory Trick
Essential amino acids: "PVT TIM HALL" = Phe, Val, Thr, Trp, Ile, Met, His, Arg (conditionally), Leu, Lys.

02 Enzymes & Kinetics

Enzymes lower the activation energy of reactions without changing the equilibrium or the overall free energy change (delta-G). They speed up reactions that are already thermodynamically favorable. Most enzymes are proteins; a few are RNA (ribozymes).

Michaelis-Menten: Two Numbers That Tell You Everything

Vmax = the maximum rate when all active sites are saturated. Km = the substrate concentration at which velocity = Vmax/2. Low Km means high affinity (the enzyme reaches half-speed with very little substrate around).

Michaelis-Menten Equation
v = (Vmax x [S]) / (Km + [S])
Worked Example: Comparing Two Enzymes

Enzyme A: Km = 0.1 mM, Vmax = 100 umol/min. Enzyme B: Km = 10 mM, Vmax = 100 umol/min. Both are in a solution with [S] = 0.1 mM. Which enzyme is working faster?

1.Enzyme A: v = (100 x 0.1) / (0.1 + 0.1) = 10 / 0.2 = 50 umol/min (half of Vmax, as expected since [S] = Km).
2.Enzyme B: v = (100 x 0.1) / (10 + 0.1) = 10 / 10.1 = 0.99 umol/min (barely working because [S] is way below its Km).
3.Enzyme A is 50 times faster at this substrate concentration. Same Vmax, but the lower Km gives it a massive advantage at low [S].

This is exactly the kind of question that shows why Km matters clinically.

Inhibition: What Changes and What Stays the Same

TypeKmVmaxOvercome by more [S]?
CompetitiveIncreases (apparent)UnchangedYes
NoncompetitiveUnchangedDecreasesNo
UncompetitiveDecreasesDecreasesNo
Worked Example: Identifying Inhibition Type

You run a kinetics experiment. Without inhibitor: Km = 5 mM, Vmax = 200. With inhibitor: Km = 5 mM, Vmax = 100. What type?

1.Km is unchanged (still 5 mM).
2.Vmax decreased (from 200 to 100).
3.Km same + Vmax down = Noncompetitive inhibition.
Memory Trick
Competitive: the inhibitor competes for the active site, so you need more substrate to overcome it (Km goes up). Noncompetitive: inhibitor binds elsewhere and breaks the enzyme (Vmax goes down, Km unaffected).

03 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates follow the general formula Cₙ(H₂O)ₙ. They serve as energy sources (glucose, glycogen), structural molecules (cellulose, chitin), and cell surface markers. The simplest carbohydrates are monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose). Two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond form a disaccharide.

Key Disaccharides

DisaccharideComponentsBondReducing?
MaltoseGlucose + Glucosealpha-1,4Yes
LactoseGalactose + Glucosebeta-1,4Yes
SucroseGlucose + Fructosealpha-1,2No
Why Sucrose Is Non-Reducing
A reducing sugar has a free anomeric carbon that can open and donate electrons. In sucrose, both anomeric carbons (C1 of glucose and C2 of fructose) are locked in the glycosidic bond, so neither is free. Maltose and lactose each have one free anomeric carbon, making them reducing sugars.

Polysaccharides

Starch (amylose + amylopectin) = plant storage, alpha-1,4 linkages (with alpha-1,6 branches in amylopectin). Humans digest it easily. Glycogen = animal storage, like amylopectin but more highly branched (branches every 8-12 residues). Cellulose = plant structural, beta-1,4 linkages. Humans cannot digest it because we lack the enzyme (cellulase) to break beta-1,4 bonds.

Same Sugar, Different Linkage

Starch and cellulose are both polymers of glucose. The only difference is alpha-1,4 (starch, digestible) versus beta-1,4 (cellulose, indigestible). One bond orientation is the difference between food and fiber.

04 Lipids & Membranes

Lipids are hydrophobic or amphipathic molecules. The major categories are fatty acids, triacylglycerols (energy storage), phospholipids (membranes), steroids (cholesterol, hormones), and sphingolipids.

Fatty Acids

Saturated = no double bonds, straight chains, pack tightly, solid at room temperature (butter, animal fat). Unsaturated = one or more double bonds create kinks, cannot pack as tightly, liquid at room temperature (olive oil, fish oil). More unsaturation = lower melting point = more fluid.

Why Cold-Water Fish Have More Unsaturated Fats

At cold temperatures, saturated fats would solidify and membranes would become rigid. Cold-water fish incorporate more unsaturated fatty acids into their membranes to maintain fluidity at low temperatures. This is the same reason butter (saturated) is solid in your fridge but olive oil (unsaturated) stays liquid.

Cholesterol in Membranes

Cholesterol is a fluidity buffer. At high temperatures, it restricts movement and reduces fluidity. At low temperatures, it prevents tight packing and increases fluidity. It keeps the membrane in a functional middle ground.

Energy Density
Fat: 9 kcal/g | Carbohydrate: 4 kcal/g | Protein: 4 kcal/g

Fat stores more than twice the energy per gram because fatty acids are more reduced (more C-H bonds to oxidize) and stored anhydrously (without water), unlike glycogen which binds ~3g water per gram.

05 Glycolysis

Glycolysis converts one glucose (6 carbons) into two pyruvate (3 carbons each) in the cytoplasm. It does not require oxygen. Net yield: 2 ATP and 2 NADH per glucose.

The Three Irreversible Steps (Rate-Limiting Enzymes)

StepEnzymeWhat It DoesRegulation
1HexokinaseGlucose → G6P (traps glucose in cell)Inhibited by G6P
3PFK-1 (rate-limiting)F6P → F-1,6-BP (committed step)Activated by AMP, F-2,6-BP. Inhibited by ATP, citrate.
10Pyruvate kinasePEP → PyruvateActivated by F-1,6-BP. Inhibited by ATP, alanine.
Why Lactate? The NAD+ Problem

Glycolysis needs NAD+ to keep running (step 6 uses it). Normally, the mitochondria regenerate NAD+ via the electron transport chain. But without oxygen (anaerobic conditions):

1.The ETC cannot run, so NADH piles up and NAD+ runs out.
2.Lactate dehydrogenase converts pyruvate to lactate, regenerating NAD+ in the process.
3.This lets glycolysis continue producing 2 ATP per glucose, which is not much but keeps you alive during a sprint.
Memory Trick: PDH Coenzymes
"Tender Loving Care For Nancy" = TPP, Lipoic acid, CoA, FAD, NAD+. Pyruvate dehydrogenase (the bridge between glycolysis and TCA) uses all five coenzymes.

06 TCA Cycle & Oxidative Phosphorylation

The TCA Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle / Krebs Cycle)

Acetyl-CoA (2C) enters by combining with oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C). Each turn releases 2 CO₂ and harvests electrons as 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 GTP. The main purpose of TCA is to generate electron carriers (NADH and FADH₂) for the electron transport chain.

TCA Intermediates in Order
"Can I Keep Selling Succinyl For Malate, Officer?" = Citrate, Isocitrate, alpha-Ketoglutarate, Succinyl-CoA, Succinate, Fumarate, Malate, Oxaloacetate.

Oxidative Phosphorylation (ETC + ATP Synthase)

NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the electron transport chain in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As electrons flow through Complexes I, III, and IV, protons (H+) are pumped into the intermembrane space, creating an electrochemical gradient. ATP synthase uses this gradient to make ATP.

This is why FADH₂ yields ~1.5 ATP while NADH yields ~2.5 ATP. FADH₂ enters at Complex II, which skips the proton pumping that Complex I performs.
Worked Example: Total ATP from One Glucose
1.Glycolysis: 2 ATP + 2 NADH (each NADH = ~2.5 ATP via malate-aspartate shuttle) = 2 + 5 = 7
2.PDH: 2 NADH (one per pyruvate) = 5 ATP
3.TCA (x2 turns): 6 NADH (= 15 ATP) + 2 FADH₂ (= 3 ATP) + 2 GTP (= 2 ATP) = 20
4.Total: 7 + 5 + 20 = ~30-32 ATP
Diagnostic Reasoning: Inhibitor vs. Uncoupler

Scenario: You measure a cell and find oxygen consumption is HIGH but ATP production is LOW. What is happening?

1.An ETC inhibitor (like cyanide) would stop electron flow, so oxygen consumption would DROP (electrons cannot reach O₂).
2.An uncoupler (like DNP or thermogenin) lets protons leak across the membrane, collapsing the gradient. The ETC runs FASTER trying to restore it (more O₂ consumed), but ATP synthase has no gradient to use (less ATP).
3.Answer: uncoupler. O₂ consumption up + ATP down = energy wasted as heat.

07 Fat Metabolism & Ketone Bodies

Beta-Oxidation (Breaking Down Fat)

Fatty acids are broken down 2 carbons at a time in the mitochondrial matrix, producing acetyl-CoA, NADH, and FADH₂ each round. A 16-carbon fatty acid (palmitate) goes through 7 rounds and produces 8 acetyl-CoA.

Rounds of Beta-Oxidation
Rounds = (carbons / 2) - 1. For palmitate (16C): (16/2) - 1 = 7 rounds.
Worked Example: ATP from Palmitate
1.7 rounds produce 7 NADH (= 17.5 ATP) + 7 FADH₂ (= 10.5 ATP) = 28 ATP from the rounds.
2.8 acetyl-CoA enter TCA: each produces 10 ATP (3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP) = 80 ATP.
3.Subtract 2 ATP for initial activation (fatty acyl-CoA synthetase).
4.Total: 28 + 80 - 2 = 106 ATP from one palmitate.

Compare to ~30-32 from glucose. Fat is a far more efficient energy source per molecule.

Ketone Bodies

When fat breakdown is high and carbohydrate availability is low (fasting, starvation, uncontrolled diabetes), the liver produces ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone) from excess acetyl-CoA. The brain, which normally uses only glucose, can adapt to use ketone bodies during prolonged fasting.

The Liver Makes Ketones But Cannot Use Them
The liver lacks thiophorase (succinyl-CoA:acetoacetate CoA transferase), the enzyme needed to convert ketone bodies back to acetyl-CoA. So the liver exports them for other tissues (brain, heart, muscle) to use.

CPT-I: The Gatekeeper

Carnitine palmitoyltransferase I (CPT-I) is the rate-limiting step of beta-oxidation. It moves fatty acids into the mitochondria. It is inhibited by malonyl-CoA (the first committed intermediate of fatty acid synthesis). This makes sense: when you are building fat, you should not simultaneously be burning it.

08 Fed vs. Fasted: Metabolic Integration

The key to metabolic regulation is understanding two hormones: insulin (released when blood glucose is HIGH, after eating) and glucagon (released when blood glucose is LOW, during fasting).

StateDominant HormoneWhat Happens
FedInsulinGlucose uptake, glycolysis, glycogenesis, fatty acid synthesis, protein synthesis. Storage mode.
FastingGlucagonGlycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, beta-oxidation, ketogenesis. Mobilization mode.
Worked Example: What Happens Hour by Hour During Fasting
0-4h:Blood glucose maintained by liver glycogen breakdown (glycogenolysis).
4-16h:Glycogen depleted. Liver switches to gluconeogenesis (making glucose from lactate, amino acids, glycerol).
16-48h:Fat mobilization increases. Beta-oxidation provides acetyl-CoA. Liver makes ketone bodies.
>48h:Brain adapts to use ketone bodies, sparing glucose and reducing the need to break down muscle protein for gluconeogenesis.
Worked Example: Why DKA Is Dangerous

In Type 1 diabetes, no insulin is produced. Even though blood glucose is HIGH, cells cannot take it up.

1.Without insulin, cells think they are starving. Glucagon signals dominate.
2.Massive lipolysis releases fatty acids. Liver goes into overdrive with beta-oxidation.
3.Excess acetyl-CoA overwhelms TCA. Liver converts it to ketone bodies.
4.Ketone bodies are acids. Blood pH drops (ketoacidosis). This is life-threatening.

Every pathway is behaving logically. The problem is the missing insulin signal that would normally stop all of this.

Organ-Specific Rules

Muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase, so it cannot export free glucose. Muscle glycogen is for muscle use only. Liver has glucose-6-phosphatase and can export glucose to maintain blood levels for the whole body. Brain normally uses only glucose (fatty acids cannot cross the blood-brain barrier) but adapts to ketone bodies during prolonged fasting. Red blood cells have no mitochondria, so they rely entirely on glycolysis (anaerobic, producing lactate).

The Cori Cycle
Muscle sends lactate to the liver. Liver converts lactate to glucose via gluconeogenesis. Liver sends glucose back to muscle. This cycle costs the liver 6 ATP but provides the muscle with 2 ATP per cycle, a net loss that the liver absorbs to keep muscle running.
