01 Amino Acids & Protein Structure

There are 20 amino acids. They all share the same backbone (amino group, carboxyl group, alpha carbon), but each has a unique side chain (R-group) that determines its properties. The R-group is everything. It controls whether the amino acid is hydrophobic, polar, positively charged, or negatively charged.

The Four R-Group Categories

GroupCharacterExamples
NonpolarOily, avoids water, buries inside proteinsAla (A), Val (V), Leu (L), Ile (I), Phe (F), Pro (P)
Polar unchargedCan hydrogen-bond with water, no formal chargeSer (S), Thr (T), Asn (N), Gln (Q), Cys (C), Tyr (Y)
Positive (+) at pH 7.4Basic side chains, carry positive charge at body pHLys (K, pKa ~10.5), Arg (R, pKa ~12.5), His (H, pKa ~6.0)
Negative (−) at pH 7.4Acidic side chains, carry negative charge at body pHAsp (D, pKa ~3.65), Glu (E, pKa ~4.25)
Common Mistake: "Nonpolar" ≠ "Uncharged"
These are two separate categories and students routinely confuse them. Serine is polar and uncharged: it has no formal charge but its —OH group still hydrogen-bonds with water. Leucine is nonpolar: it cannot even do that. It actively repels water. Many exam questions hinge on this distinction.

Calculating the Isoelectric Point (pI)

The pI is the pH at which an amino acid has zero net charge. The rule: average the two pKa values that bracket the neutral (zwitterion) form.

For a simple amino acid with no ionizable side chain, that is always pKa1 (carboxyl) and pKa2 (amino). For amino acids with ionizable side chains, the rule shifts depending on whether the side chain is acidic or basic.

Worked Example: pI of Glycine (simple amino acid)

Glycine: pKa1 = 2.34 (carboxyl), pKa2 = 9.60 (amino). No ionizable side chain.

1.The zwitterion (net zero) is bracketed by pKa1 and pKa2.
2.pI = (2.34 + 9.60) / 2 = 5.97

At pH 5.97, glycine carries no net charge.

Worked Example: pI of Aspartate (acidic amino acid)

Asp: pKa1 = 2.10 (backbone carboxyl), pKaR = 3.86 (side chain carboxyl), pKa2 = 9.82 (amino).

1.Asp has an extra acidic group. The zwitterion is bracketed by the two lowest pKa values.
2.pI = (2.10 + 3.86) / 2 = 2.98

Rule to remember: Acidic amino acids (Asp, Glu) have low pI — average the two lowest pKa values. Basic amino acids (Lys, Arg, His) have high pI — average the two highest pKa values.

Protein Structure Levels

Primary = the amino acid sequence itself. Secondary = local folding patterns (alpha-helix and beta-sheet) held by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms. Tertiary = the overall 3D shape of one polypeptide, driven mainly by hydrophobic interactions burying nonpolar residues inside. Quaternary = multiple polypeptide subunits assembled together (like hemoglobin, which has four subunits).

Worked Example: Sickle Cell Disease Step by Step

Sickle cell is caused by one amino acid change: glutamate (charged, hydrophilic) to valine (nonpolar, hydrophobic) at position 6 of the beta-globin chain.

1.Glu → Val creates a hydrophobic sticky patch on the surface of hemoglobin (where it does not belong).
2.Under low O₂ (deoxy-Hb), this patch is exposed.
3.The sticky patch binds to a complementary pocket on another hemoglobin molecule.
4.Hemoglobin molecules chain together into long fibers, distorting the RBC into a sickle shape.

One amino acid change (primary structure) cascades into a quaternary-level aggregation problem and a clinical disease. This is the clearest example of why primary structure matters.

Memory Trick: Essential Amino Acids
"PVT TIM HALL" = Phe, Val, Thr, Trp, Ile, Met, His, Arg (conditionally essential), Leu, Lys. These must come from diet because the body cannot synthesize them.

02 Enzymes & Kinetics

Enzymes lower the activation energy of reactions without changing the equilibrium or the overall free energy change (ΔG). They speed up reactions that are already thermodynamically favorable but would otherwise be too slow. Most enzymes are proteins; a few are RNA (ribozymes).

Michaelis-Menten: Two Numbers That Tell You Everything

Vmax = the maximum reaction rate when every active site is saturated with substrate. Km = the substrate concentration at which velocity equals exactly Vmax/2. Think of Km as a measure of affinity: a low Km means the enzyme grabs its substrate tightly and reaches half-speed with very little substrate around. A high Km means the enzyme is a weak binder and needs a lot of substrate to work efficiently.

Michaelis-Menten Equation
v = (Vmax × [S]) / (Km + [S])
Worked Example: Comparing Two Enzymes

Enzyme A: Km = 0.1 mM, Vmax = 100 µmol/min. Enzyme B: Km = 10 mM, Vmax = 100 µmol/min. Both are in a solution with [S] = 0.1 mM. Which is working faster?

1.Enzyme A: v = (100 × 0.1) / (0.1 + 0.1) = 10 / 0.2 = 50 µmol/min (half of Vmax — expected because [S] = Km).
2.Enzyme B: v = (100 × 0.1) / (10 + 0.1) = 10 / 10.1 = ~1 µmol/min (barely moving because [S] is far below its Km).
3.Same Vmax, but Enzyme A is ~50× faster at this concentration because its lower Km gives it a huge advantage when substrate is scarce.

Enzyme Inhibition: What Changes and What Stays the Same

There are three types of reversible inhibition you must know. The key is understanding where the inhibitor binds and what that does to your kinetic parameters.

Competitive

Inhibitor binds the active site directly, competing with substrate for the same spot.

Because it physically blocks substrate, you need more [S] to reach Vmax — but with enough substrate, you can flood the enzyme and reach the same Vmax.

Km ↑ Vmax unchanged

Noncompetitive

Inhibitor binds an allosteric site (elsewhere on the enzyme), not the active site. Substrate can still bind normally.

But once the inhibitor is bound, catalysis is impaired regardless of how much substrate you add. No amount of extra substrate can fix it.

Km unchanged Vmax ↓

Uncompetitive

Inhibitor can only bind the enzyme-substrate complex (ES), not the free enzyme. This is the counterintuitive one.

Because it locks ES in place, substrate binds more tightly (Km drops). But catalysis is blocked, so Vmax also drops. Both go down together.

Km ↓ Vmax ↓
TypeBinds where?KmVmaxOvercome by more [S]?
Competitive Active site Increases Unchanged Yes
Noncompetitive Allosteric site Unchanged Decreases No

🔎 How to Identify the Inhibitor Type from Data
Step 1→Look at Vmax first. Did it change?
→Vmax unchanged? It is Competitive. Done.
→Vmax decreased? Go to Step 2.
Step 2→Now look at Km. Did it change?
→Km unchanged? It is Noncompetitive.
→Km decreased? It is Uncompetitive.
Worked Example: Identifying Inhibition Type from Data

Without inhibitor: Km = 5 mM, Vmax = 200. With inhibitor: Km = 5 mM, Vmax = 100. What type?

1.Check Vmax: decreased from 200 → 100. So it is not competitive (competitive leaves Vmax unchanged).
2.Check Km: unchanged (still 5 mM). So it is not uncompetitive (uncompetitive decreases Km).
3.Vmax down + Km unchanged = Noncompetitive inhibition.

The inhibitor is binding an allosteric site. Substrate still binds normally (Km unchanged), but catalysis is impaired (Vmax down). No amount of extra substrate will restore full activity.

Memory Trick: One Sentence Per Type
Competitive: "Same site, more substrate wins." (Active site, Km up, Vmax okay.)
Noncompetitive: "Different site, catalysis broken." (Allosteric site, Km fine, Vmax down.)
Uncompetitive: "Only grabs when substrate is already there. Both go down." (Km down, Vmax down.)
The Uncompetitive Trap: Why Does Km Decrease?
Students expect Km to stay the same or go up. Here is why it drops: the inhibitor can only bind the ES complex, which means it stabilizes the ES complex and prevents substrate from leaving. This makes it look like the enzyme is binding substrate more tightly (lower apparent Km). But the complex is then stuck and cannot complete the reaction (Vmax drops). Both parameters fall proportionally, which is the diagnostic signature.

03 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates follow the general formula Cₙ(H₂O)ₙ. They serve as energy sources (glucose, glycogen), structural molecules (cellulose, chitin), and cell surface markers. The simplest carbohydrates are monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose). Two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond form a disaccharide.

Key Disaccharides

DisaccharideComponentsBondReducing?
MaltoseGlucose + Glucosealpha-1,4Yes
LactoseGalactose + Glucosebeta-1,4Yes
SucroseGlucose + Fructosealpha-1,2No
Why Sucrose Is Non-Reducing
A reducing sugar has a free anomeric carbon that can open and donate electrons. In sucrose, both anomeric carbons (C1 of glucose and C2 of fructose) are locked in the glycosidic bond, so neither is free. Maltose and lactose each have one free anomeric carbon, making them reducing sugars.

Polysaccharides

Starch (amylose + amylopectin) = plant storage, alpha-1,4 linkages with alpha-1,6 branches in amylopectin. Humans digest it easily. Glycogen = animal storage, like amylopectin but more highly branched (every 8–12 residues), allowing faster glucose release. Cellulose = plant structural, beta-1,4 linkages. Humans cannot digest it because we lack cellulase to break beta-1,4 bonds.

Same Sugar, Different Linkage = Completely Different Function

Starch and cellulose are both polymers of glucose. The only structural difference is alpha-1,4 (starch, digestible) vs. beta-1,4 (cellulose, indigestible). One bond orientation is the difference between food and fiber.

04 Lipids & Membranes

Lipids are hydrophobic or amphipathic molecules. The major categories are fatty acids, triacylglycerols (energy storage), phospholipids (membranes), steroids (cholesterol, hormones), and sphingolipids.

Fatty Acids: Saturated vs. Unsaturated

Saturated = no double bonds, straight chains, pack tightly together, solid at room temperature (butter, animal fat). Unsaturated = one or more double bonds create kinks in the chain, cannot pack tightly, liquid at room temperature (olive oil, fish oil). More unsaturation = lower melting point = more fluid membrane.

Why Cold-Water Fish Have More Unsaturated Fats

At cold temperatures, saturated fats would solidify and membranes would become rigid and non-functional. Cold-water fish incorporate more unsaturated fatty acids into their membranes to maintain fluidity at low temperatures. This is the same reason butter (saturated) is solid in your fridge while olive oil (unsaturated) stays liquid.

Cholesterol: The Fluidity Buffer

Cholesterol inserts between phospholipid tails in the membrane. At high temperatures, it restricts movement and reduces fluidity. At low temperatures, it prevents tight packing and increases fluidity. It keeps the membrane in the functional middle ground at both extremes.

Energy Density (per gram)
Fat: 9 kcal/g  |  Carbohydrate: 4 kcal/g  |  Protein: 4 kcal/g

Fat stores more than twice the energy per gram because fatty acids are more reduced (more C–H bonds to oxidize) and stored without water, unlike glycogen which binds ~3 g water per gram of glycogen.

05 Glycolysis

Glycolysis converts one glucose (6C) into two pyruvate (3C each) in the cytoplasm. It does not require oxygen. Net yield: 2 ATP and 2 NADH per glucose. There is a trick: the first half of glycolysis costs 2 ATP (investment phase). The second half produces 4 ATP. Net = 2 ATP.

The Three Irreversible Steps (Rate-Limiting Enzymes)

StepEnzymeWhat It DoesRegulation
1HexokinaseGlucose → G6P (traps glucose inside the cell)Inhibited by its product, G6P
3PFK-1 (rate-limiting)F6P → F-1,6-BP (the committed step — no turning back)Activated by AMP, F-2,6-BP. Inhibited by ATP, citrate.
10Pyruvate kinasePEP → Pyruvate (final ATP-generating step)Activated by F-1,6-BP. Inhibited by ATP, alanine.
Why PFK-1 Is the True Rate-Limiting Step
Hexokinase acts first, but PFK-1 is the committed step. Once F-1,6-bisphosphate is made, the cell is fully committed to completing glycolysis. Before that point, G6P could be diverted to glycogen synthesis or the pentose phosphate pathway. PFK-1 is also the most heavily regulated of the three enzymes, which is why it controls overall flux.
Why Lactate? The NAD+ Problem

Glycolysis needs NAD+ at step 6 (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase). Normally, the mitochondria regenerate NAD+ from NADH via the electron transport chain. But without oxygen:

1.The ETC cannot run. NADH accumulates and NAD+ runs out.
2.Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) converts pyruvate → lactate, simultaneously converting NADH back to NAD+.
3.This recycled NAD+ keeps glycolysis running, producing 2 ATP per glucose. Not much, but enough to sustain a sprint.

Lactate is not a metabolic dead end. It is exported to the liver (Cori cycle) and converted back to glucose.

Memory Trick: PDH Coenzymes
"Tender Loving Care For Nancy" = TPP, Lipoic acid, CoA, FAD, NAD+. Pyruvate dehydrogenase (the bridge between glycolysis and TCA) requires all five.

06 TCA Cycle & Oxidative Phosphorylation

The TCA Cycle: What It Actually Does

Students often think the TCA cycle makes ATP. It mostly does not. Its real job is to strip electrons from carbon compounds and load them onto electron carriers (NADH and FADH₂). Those carriers then take the electrons to the ETC, where the real ATP is made. Each turn of the cycle releases 2 CO₂ and produces 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 GTP.

TCA Intermediates in Order
"Can I Keep Selling Succinyl For Malate, Officer?"
Citrate → Isocitrate → α-Ketoglutarate → Succinyl-CoA → Succinate → Fumarate → Malate → Oxaloacetate

Oxidative Phosphorylation: The ETC + ATP Synthase

NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the electron transport chain in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As electrons flow through the complexes, protons (H+) are pumped into the intermembrane space, creating an electrochemical gradient. ATP synthase uses the flow of protons back across the membrane to make ATP.

Complex II Does NOT Pump Protons — This Matters for ATP Yield
FADH₂ enters at Complex II, which transfers electrons to CoQ but does not pump protons. This means FADH₂ contributes less to the proton gradient. Result: NADH yields ~2.5 ATP; FADH₂ yields only ~1.5 ATP.
Worked Example: Total ATP from One Glucose
1.Glycolysis: 2 ATP (direct) + 2 NADH (≈ 5 ATP via malate-aspartate shuttle) = 7 ATP
2.PDH bridge (2 pyruvates): 2 NADH ≈ 5 ATP
3.TCA (2 turns): 6 NADH (≈15 ATP) + 2 FADH₂ (≈3 ATP) + 2 GTP = 20 ATP
4.Total: 7 + 5 + 20 = ~30–32 ATP

Modern values use P/O ratios (NADH = 2.5, FADH₂ = 1.5) rather than the old textbook values of 3 and 2. Expect to see ~30–32 on exams, not the outdated "36–38."

Worked Example: Inhibitor vs. Uncoupler — A Classic Exam Scenario

You measure a cell: oxygen consumption is HIGH, but ATP production is LOW. What is happening?

1.An ETC inhibitor (like cyanide) would block electron flow. Electrons cannot reach O₂, so oxygen consumption would drop. That is the opposite of what we see.
2.An uncoupler (like DNP or thermogenin) pokes holes in the inner membrane, letting protons leak back across without going through ATP synthase. The gradient collapses.
3.The ETC runs faster trying to restore the gradient (more O₂ consumed), but ATP synthase has no gradient to use (no ATP made). Energy is wasted as heat.
4.Answer: Uncoupler. O₂ up + ATP down = energy dissipated as heat.

Thermogenin (UCP1) is the body's natural uncoupler in brown adipose tissue. It is how newborns and hibernating animals generate heat.

07 Fat Metabolism & Ketone Bodies

Beta-Oxidation: Breaking Down Fat

Fatty acids are broken down 2 carbons at a time in the mitochondrial matrix. Each round produces one acetyl-CoA, one NADH, and one FADH₂. The acetyl-CoA enters the TCA cycle. A 16-carbon fatty acid (palmitate) undergoes 7 rounds and produces 8 acetyl-CoA.

How Many Rounds of Beta-Oxidation?
Rounds = (number of carbons ÷ 2) − 1    Palmitate (16C): (16/2) − 1 = 7 rounds
Worked Example: ATP from Palmitate
1.Activation cost: −2 ATP (fatty acyl-CoA synthetase uses ATP → AMP + 2Pi)
2.7 rounds of beta-oxidation: 7 NADH (≈17.5 ATP) + 7 FADH₂ (≈10.5 ATP) = 28 ATP
3.8 acetyl-CoA → TCA: each yields ~10 ATP = 80 ATP
4.Total: 28 + 80 − 2 = ~106 ATP

Compare to ~30–32 ATP from glucose. Fat yields far more ATP per molecule, which is why it is the body's preferred long-term fuel storage form.

Ketone Bodies: Fuel When Glucose Is Low

When fat breakdown is high and carbohydrate availability is low (fasting, starvation, uncontrolled diabetes), the liver produces ketone bodies from excess acetyl-CoA. The three ketone bodies are acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, and acetone. The brain, which normally uses only glucose, can adapt to use ketone bodies during prolonged fasting.

The Liver Makes Ketones But Cannot Use Them
The liver lacks thiophorase (succinyl-CoA:acetoacetate CoA transferase), the enzyme needed to convert ketone bodies back to acetyl-CoA for the TCA cycle. So the liver exports ketones for the brain, heart, and muscle to use. This is a classic exam question.

CPT-I: The Gatekeeper of Beta-Oxidation

Carnitine palmitoyltransferase I (CPT-I) is rate-limiting for beta-oxidation. It transfers fatty acids from the cytoplasm into the mitochondria. It is inhibited by malonyl-CoA, the first committed intermediate of fatty acid synthesis. The logic: when you are building fat (high malonyl-CoA), you should not simultaneously be burning it. This is a beautiful example of metabolic logic built into chemistry.

08 Fed vs. Fasted: Metabolic Integration

All of metabolic regulation comes down to two hormones: insulin (released when blood glucose is high, after eating) and glucagon (released when blood glucose is low, during fasting). Every pathway you have learned is either turned on or off by one of these two signals.

StateDominant HormoneWhat Happens
FedInsulinGlucose uptake, glycolysis, glycogenesis, fatty acid synthesis, protein synthesis. The body is in storage/building mode.
FastingGlucagonGlycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, beta-oxidation, ketogenesis. The body is in mobilization mode.
Worked Example: What Happens Hour by Hour During a 72-Hour Fast
0–4hBlood glucose maintained by liver glycogen breakdown (glycogenolysis). Liver has ~100g glycogen, enough for about 4–6 hours.
4–16hLiver glycogen depletes. Liver switches to gluconeogenesis, making new glucose from lactate, amino acids, and glycerol.
16–48hFat mobilization increases. Beta-oxidation provides acetyl-CoA. Liver produces ketone bodies as excess acetyl-CoA overwhelms TCA capacity.
>48hBrain adapts to use ketone bodies, reducing glucose demand. This spares muscle protein from being broken down for gluconeogenesis. The body is protecting muscle mass.
Worked Example: Why DKA Is Dangerous

In Type 1 diabetes, no insulin is produced. Blood glucose is very high, but cells cannot take it up without insulin.

1.Without insulin, cells behave as if starving even though glucose is abundant. Glucagon signals dominate.
2.Massive lipolysis releases fatty acids. The liver runs beta-oxidation at maximum rate.
3.Excess acetyl-CoA overwhelms TCA cycle capacity. The liver converts it to ketone bodies at high rate.
4.Ketone bodies are acids (pKa ~3.5). Blood pH drops (ketoacidosis). This is life-threatening.

The key insight: every pathway is behaving correctly given the signal it is receiving. The problem is the missing insulin signal that would normally shut all of this down.

Organ-Specific Rules You Must Know

Muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase. It cannot export free glucose. Muscle glycogen is for muscle use only. Liver has glucose-6-phosphatase and can release free glucose into the blood to maintain levels for all other tissues. Brain normally uses only glucose (fatty acids cannot cross the blood-brain barrier) but adapts to ketone bodies during prolonged fasting. Red blood cells have no mitochondria and rely entirely on glycolysis, producing lactate as their only byproduct.

The Cori Cycle: How Muscle and Liver Cooperate
Muscle produces lactate (from anaerobic glycolysis) → lactate travels to liver → liver converts lactate to glucose (gluconeogenesis, costs 6 ATP) → glucose travels back to muscle (used for 2 ATP in glycolysis). Net cost to the liver: 4 ATP per cycle. The liver absorbs this cost so that muscle can keep working.
